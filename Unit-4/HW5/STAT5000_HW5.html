A.1 [6 points] Expectations do not necessarily exist!¶

Unfortunately, the expected value of a random variable may not be defined. For example, if $\theta$ is an angle uniformly distributed in the interval $(-\pi/2,\pi/2)$, then $X=\alpha\tan(\theta)$ has the following distribution:

$$ f(x)=\frac{\alpha}{\pi}\frac{1}{x^2+\alpha^2} $$

Show that $E(X)$ is undefined (HINT: compute $E(X)$ using a u-substitution and show that the integral does not converge).

To find $E(X)$ note that, $$\begin{gather*} X = \alpha tan(\frac{\pi}{2}) = \infty \\ X = \alpha tan(-\frac{\pi}{2}) = -\infty \end{gather*} $$ which will be our bounds for $E(X)$ so, $$ \begin{gather*} \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} x \bigg[\frac{\alpha}{\pi} (\frac{1}{x^2 + \alpha^2})\bigg]dx \\ \frac{2\alpha}{\pi}\int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \frac{x}{x^2 + \alpha^2}dx \end{gather*} $$ Let $u = x^2 + \alpha^2$ therefore $du = 2x dx$. Hence:

$$\begin{gather*} \frac{\alpha}{\pi}\int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \frac{1}{u}du \\ \frac{\alpha}{\pi}\bigg[\ln{\big(x^2 + \alpha^2\big)}\bigg]^{\infty}_{-\infty} \\ \end{gather*}$$

So if we let $a \rightarrow \infty$ and $b \rightarrow \infty$ we can take the limit of, $$ \frac{\alpha}{\pi}\bigg[\ln{\big(x^2 + \alpha^2\big)}\bigg]^{a \rightarrow \infty}_{b \rightarrow -\infty} $$ Hence, $$ \begin{gather*} \lim_{a\rightarrow \infty} \ln(a^2 + \alpha^2) = \infty \\ \lim_{b\rightarrow -\infty} \ln(b^2 + \alpha^2) = \infty \end{gather*} $$ Ergo, $$\begin{gather*} \frac{\alpha}{\pi}\bigg[\ln{\big(x^2 + \alpha^2\big)}\bigg]^{a \rightarrow \infty}_{b \rightarrow -\infty} = \infty \end{gather*}$$

A.2 Properties of expectation¶

Let $X$ be a random variable such that

  • $E(X)=5$
  • $sd(X)=2.5$

and let $Y$ be a random variable such that

  • $E(Y)=8$,
  • $sd(Y)=2$.

$X$ and $Y$ are not independent. In fact, $corr(X,Y)=0.8$.

Compute the following.

A.2(a) [3 points] $E(4X+3Y+1)$

$E(4X + 3Y + 1) = 4E(X) + 3E(Y) + 1 = 4(5) + 3(8) + 1 = 45$

A.2(b) [6 points] $sd(4X+2Y+2)$

$$\begin{gather*} sd(4X+2Y+2) = \sqrt{Var(4X + 2Y + 2)} \\ = \sqrt{4^2\cdot Var(X) + 2^2\cdot Var(Y) + 2\cdot4\cdot2\cdot Cov(X,Y)} \\ = \sqrt{16(6.25) + 4(4) + 2\cdot4\cdot3\cdot4} \\ = \sqrt{100 + 16 + 64} \\ = 13.41 \end{gather*}$$

A.2(c) [4 points] $E(3X^2 + Y)$

We know $sd(X) = \sqrt{(Var(X)}$. We also know $Var(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2$

Therefore $sd(X) = 2.5 = \sqrt{6.25}$, so $Var(X) = 6.25$. From above we now have: $$6.25 = E(X^2) - 25 \Rightarrow E(X^2) = 31.25$$

Ergo, $$E(3X^2 + Y) = 3E(X^2) + E(Y) = 3(31.25) + 8 = 101.75$$

A.2(d) [4 points] $E(8XY)$

We know that $Corr(X,Y) = \frac{Cov(X,Y)}{sd(X)sd(Y)}$ and $Cov(X,Y) = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y)$

We are given that $Corr(X,Y)=0.8$ therefore: $$0.8 = \frac{Cov(X,Y)}{sd(X)sd(Y)} \Rightarrow 0.8 = \frac{E(XY) - E(X)E(Y)}{sd(X)sd(Y)}$$

Now plug in, $$0.8 = \frac{E(XY) - 5(8)}{(2.5)2} \Rightarrow 0.8 = \frac{E(XY) - 40}{5} \newline \Rightarrow E(XY) = 352$$

A.3 [4 points] Covariance and independence¶

Suppose that X and Y are independent random variables. Prove that $E(XY) = E(X)E(Y)$.

For a continuous r.v., $X$ and $Y$: $$E(XY) = E\big(g(x)h(y)\big) = \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}g(x)h(y)f_x(x)f_y(y)dxdy \newline \Rightarrow \bigg(\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}g(x)f_x(x)dx\bigg)\bigg(\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}h(y)f_y(y)dy\bigg)\newline \Rightarrow E\big(g(x)\big)E\big(h(y)\big) \newline \Rightarrow E(X)E(Y)$$

For a discrete r.v., $X$ and $Y$: $$\begin{gather*} E(XY) = E\big(g(x)h(y)\big) \\ = \sum_x\sum_y g(x)h(y)P(X)P(Y) \\ = \bigg(\sum_x g(x)P(X)\bigg)\bigg(\sum_yh(y)P(Y)\bigg) \\ = E\big(g(x)\big)E\big(h(y)\big) \\ = E(X)E(Y) \end{gather*}$$

A.4 Properties of $\bar{X}$¶

Let $ X_{1},X_{2},...,X_{n} \overset{iid}{\sim} N( \mu , \sigma^{2})$.

A.4(a) [4 points] Show (without integration), that $sd(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$

$$ \begin{gather*} Var(\bar{X}) = Var\bigg(\frac{1}{n}\sum^n_{i=1}X_i\bigg) \\ Var(\bar{X})= \frac{1}{n^2}Var\bigg(sum^n_{i=1} X_i \bigg) \\ Var(\bar{X}) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum^n_{i=1} Var\big(X_i\big) \\ Var(\bar{X}) = \frac{1}{n^2}\cdot \sigma^2n \\ Var(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \end{gather*} $$

And $sd(\bar{X}) = \sqrt{Var(\bar{X})}$: $$sd(\bar{X}) = \sqrt{Var(\bar{X})} = \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}{n}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

A.4(b) [5 points] Show that $\displaystyle\bar{X} = \mu + \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}Z$ where $Z \sim N(0,1)$.

We know $Z\sim N(0,1)$ which also mean that $Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{sd(\bar{X})}$ so: $$\begin{gather*} Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{sd(\bar{X})} \\ Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \\ {\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}}Z = \bar{X} - \mu \\ \bar{X} = \mu + {\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}}Z \end{gather*}$$

A.4.(c) [7 points] Use A.4(b) to find $\displaystyle Var(\bar{X}^{2})$. Assume that $Cov(Z, Z^2) = 0$ (it's true, but you don't have to prove it!).

$$ \begin{gather*} \bar{X}^2 = \big(\mu + \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\cdot Z\big)^2 \\ \bar{X}^2 = \mu^2 + \frac{2\mu\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\cdot Z + \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\cdot Z^2 \end{gather*}$$

Taking $Var(\bar{X}^2)$: $$ \begin{gather*} Var(\bar{X}^2) = Var\bigg(\mu^2 + \frac{2\mu\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\cdot Z + \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\cdot Z^2\bigg) \\ = \bigg(2\frac{\mu\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\bigg)^2\cdot Var(Z) + \bigg(\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\bigg)^2 \cdot Var(Z^2) + 2\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot Cov(Z,Z^2) \\ = \frac{4\mu^2\sigma^2}{n} + \frac{2\sigma^4}{n^2} \end{gather*}$$

$X_f$ and $X_s$ are note iid so to find our average we know, $$\begin{gather*} \bar{X} = \frac{1}{2}\sum^2_{i=1} X_i \\ E(\bar{X}) = \frac{1}{2}\sum^2_{i=1} E(X_i) \\ = \frac{1}{2}\big[E(X_f) + E(X_s)\big] \\ = \frac{1}{2}\big[110 + 140\big] \\ E(\bar{X}) = 125 \end{gather*}$$

Similarly for $Var(\bar{X}$: $$\begin{gather*} Var(\bar{X}) = \bigg(\frac{1}{2}\bigg)^2\cdot Var(X_f + X_s) \\ = \bigg(\frac{1}{2}\bigg)^2\big[Var(X_f + X_s)\big] + 2\cdot\bigg(\frac{1}{2}\bigg)^2\cdot Cov(X_f, X_s) \\ = \frac{\sigma^2_f + \sigma^2_s}{4} + \frac{1}{2}\big(Cov(X_f, X_s)\big) \end{gather*} $$

The distribution is still normal, the shape doesn't change. The variable that changes is the mean.

A.5(c) [5 points] Assume that Franklin’s and Sue Ellen’s scores are independent. What is the probability that the sample average score of the group will be between 120 and 140 points?

$$\begin{gather*} P(120 \leq Z \leq 140) \\ = P(\frac{120 - 125}{\sqrt{33.75}}\leq Z \leq \frac{140 - 125}{\sqrt{33.75}}) \\ = P(-0.86 \leq Z \leq 2.58) \\ = P(Z \leq 2.58) - P(Z \leq -0.86) \\ \approx 0.8003 \end{gather*} $$

A.5(d) [5 points] Assume that Franklin’s and Sue Ellen’s scores are independent. What is the probability that both players bowl a score less than 135?
